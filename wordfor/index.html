Overview

/01

Translation tools often fail to convey the nuances of emotion and regional context, leaving multilingual speakers frustrated.

WordFor, a web-app that emphasizes cultural and contextual translation accuracy, was designed to address gaps in existing translation apps. WordFor offers users the ability to translate sentiments and emotions with regional and contextual considerations—bridging the gap between languages.

Over a 6-month span, our team of five tackled wireframing, prototyping, UX research, and development, creating an intuitive and culturally responsive solution. By leveraging OpenAI’s GPT models and rigorous usability testing, we crafted an experience that received overwhelmingly positive feedback and outperformed competitors in translation accuracy.

Try it for yourself @ wordfor.vercel.app!

Problem

/02

Multilingual speakers have difficulty expressing feelings and sentiments across their respective languages.

Through a combination of surveys and interviews with multilingual people, we were able to flesh out our understanding of the problem more thoroughly.

50 people surveyed

Click to reveal how these stats breakdown.

Interviews with multilingual participants revealed more findings as to why this problem occurs, including the following:

1. There are very few culturally accurate translation resources.

2. There is a lack of direct translations for specific sentiments.

3. It is difficult to switch back and forth between languages.

How might we allow multilingual speakers to better express themselves by accounting for cultural, contextual, regional, and sentimental nuances?

Research + Features

/03

Competitive analysis

While each platform succeeds at something, WordFor strives to achieve accuracy, contextual awareness, and cultural understanding in all of its translations.

Context

Accuracy

Cultural Understanding

Based on surveys, interviews, and a card sort we were able to recognize our differentiating features and features that were required to compete with apps like Google Translate or Papago.

The required features were translation functionality, recent searches, and audio functionality. Audio functionality (like text to speech and speech to text) would be more difficult to implement but held value in its flexibility and speed. Users also stated that they might know how to pronounce a word, but not spell it, and vice versa.

This lead to the decision to implement speech to text as it’s own option rather than hiding it in the keyboard, as this would make it more difficult for users across all devices to use the microphone capabilities. Text to speech options are also implemented throughout the app for opportunity to hear examples of pronunciation.

Our differentiating features included providing context/region options and multiple results for each translation.

All interview participants stated that their language changes based on the region, but apps don’t accommodate that.

"[Translation Apps] don’t take the situational context into consideration."

-User Interview Participant

Providing multiple results was another way ensure accurate translations, as some words may have multiple different meanings, or one translation may not apply to the situation you are in.

“That makes sense having the multiple results. Google Translate only gives you one option, but sometimes it isn’t right.”

- User Interview Participant

However, it did not mean there was not confusion in our initial designs. Some people did not know which definition to default to. So, we adjusted out designs to break out the primary result and alternative translations.

Designs

/04

With the foundation of key features and user insights established, let's walk through the key task flow explaining why design decisions were made.

Or... see it live @ wordfor.vercel.app!

Home Page Flow

When selecting region, we realized that people often struggled to find what they were looking for, so a search was implemented to allow for users to find their region of choice quickly.

Translated Word + Feedback Flow

Because AI prompting can be finicky, we wanted to add feedback to improve the translation accuracy. Users are able to describe why something fails to translate creating better results in the future.

Menu Flow

The language in which recent searches compared to bookmarks was another key difference. Recent searches will show up in the language searched, while bookmarks will show up in the language translated to.

Filters Flow

To better account for power users, we included filters to search back on different languages, regions, or contexts easier. As it's in a drawer, regions have internal scrolling to the accordian.

Project takeaways

/06

If we had more time

We would focus on incorporating dynamic conversation translation (real time back and forth) and the accent keyboard.

Project evolution

We would look to implement more languages it could translate from and to, as well as testing different large language models for best results.

If we had a do over

To better understand user needs, we would outreach earlier to language / psychology / linguistics professors.
